## Indian Defence Procurement Frameworks

### iDEX (Innovations for Defence Excellence)

**What It Is**

iDEX is a framework established by the Ministry of Defence (MoD) in April 2018 to foster innovation in defence and aerospace. It functions as the primary interface between the Indian defence establishment and the startup/innovator ecosystem. iDEX is funded and managed by the Defence Innovation Organisation (DIO), a not-for-profit entity formed by the Department of Defence Production (DDP). DIO is jointly funded by two defence public sector undertakings: Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL) and Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL).

**How It Works**

1. **Problem Statement Release**: The Armed Forces (Army, Navy, Air Force) and the Department of Defence Production release problem statements (called "Defence India Startup Challenges" or DISC challenges) describing specific capability gaps.
2. **Application**: Startups, MSMEs, individual innovators, and R&D institutions apply through the iDEX portal (idex.gov.in) with a proposed solution.
3. **Evaluation**: Applications undergo a multi-stage evaluation: eligibility screening, technical evaluation by domain experts, and presentation to a Selection Committee.
4. **Prototype Grant**: Winners of a DISC challenge receive support of up to Rs 1.5 Crore (approximately $180,000 USD) as a prototype development grant. This is disbursed in tranches linked to milestones.
5. **Procurement Commitment**: Upon successful prototype validation, the armed force that issued the problem statement commits to procurement. This is a critical differentiator: iDEX provides a pathway from prototype to actual contract, not merely a grant.

**DISC Challenges**

Defence India Startup Challenges are released in batches. As of early 2026, over 12 rounds of DISC challenges have been released. Each round contains 10 to 30 problem statements across the three services and DDP. Naval communication problem statements have appeared in multiple rounds, including secure messaging, tactical data links, and software-defined networking for shipboard use.

**Grant Amounts and Funding**

- Standard iDEX grant: up to Rs 1.5 Crore per winner
- iDEX Prime (for more mature startups): up to Rs 10 Crore
- Additional procurement orders can follow, ranging from Rs 5 Crore to Rs 100+ Crore depending on the scale of the solution

**Eligibility**

- Indian startups (recognized by DPIIT)
- MSMEs (registered under the MSME Act)
- Individual innovators (Indian citizens)
- R&D institutions and academia (in partnership with an industry partner)
- The entity must have a majority Indian ownership (>50% shareholding by Indian nationals or Indian companies)

**Strategic Assessment for Our Product**

iDEX is the fastest entry point into Indian defence procurement. A naval secure communication product aligns directly with known problem statements around secure tactical messaging, shipboard communication systems, and shore-to-ship data transfer. The Rs 1.5 Crore grant covers initial prototype development costs. More importantly, the procurement commitment from the Navy that follows a successful DISC win can be worth Rs 50 to Rs 500 Crore for a fleet-wide deployment.

**Risks and Limitations**

- The Rs 1.5 Crore grant is modest; significant self-funding or venture funding is needed in parallel
- Prototype evaluation timelines can stretch from the stated 12 months to 18 to 24 months in practice
- iDEX procurement orders are typically limited to initial batches; full fleet-wide procurement requires transition to the standard DAP process

### NIIO (Naval Innovation and Indigenisation Organisation)

**What It Is**

NIIO is the Indian Navy's dedicated innovation arm, established in 2020. It consolidates the Navy's indigenisation efforts under a single umbrella and acts as the Navy-specific counterpart to iDEX. NIIO is headed by a two-star Admiral (Rear Admiral rank) and reports to the Vice Chief of Naval Staff (VCNS).

**Structure**

NIIO comprises two verticals:

1. **Naval Innovation Board (NIB)**: Focuses on identifying capability gaps, releasing problem statements, and evaluating innovative solutions from startups and industry.
2. **Naval Indigenisation Board (NIBd)**: Focuses on reducing import dependence by identifying components, systems, and subsystems that can be manufactured indigenously.

**Problem Statements**

NIIO releases problem statements specific to the Indian Navy. These cover a broad spectrum of naval requirements, from propulsion systems to communication and electronic warfare. Communication-related problem statements have included:

- Secure tactical messaging for shipboard use
- Underwater communication
- Software-defined radio interoperability
- Shipboard network cybersecurity
- Satellite communication optimization for bandwidth-constrained links

**How to Engage**

NIIO can be engaged through:

- The NIIO portal (official Indian Navy website section)
- The SPRINT programme (see below)
- Direct engagement through the Directorate of Naval Communications or the Technology Development Acceleration Cell (TDAC)
- Navy innovation seminars (SWAVLAMBAN, held annually)

**Strategic Assessment**

NIIO is the most direct path to naval procurement. Engaging with NIIO provides:

- Direct access to naval officers who define requirements
- Visibility into the Navy's technology roadmap
- A fast track to sea trials and operational evaluation
- A relationship channel that persists beyond individual procurement cycles

### SPRINT (Supporting Pole-vaulting in R&D through iDEX, NIIO, and TDAC)

**What It Is**

SPRINT is a joint initiative of the Indian Navy and iDEX, launched in July 2022 during the first SWAVLAMBAN naval innovation seminar. It identified 75 specific challenges (commemorating 75 years of Indian independence) that the Navy sought industry and startups to solve.

**The 75 Challenges**

The 75 SPRINT challenges covered domains including:

- **Communications and Networking**: Secure messaging, tactical data links, software-defined networking, bandwidth optimization for SATCOM
- **Cybersecurity**: Shipboard network intrusion detection, secure boot for naval IT systems, zero-trust architectures
- **Autonomous Systems**: USVs, UUVs, UAVs for naval operations
- **Sensors and EW**: Radar, sonar, electronic warfare
- **Platform Systems**: Propulsion, hull monitoring, damage control

Several of these challenges map directly to a naval secure communication product:

- Challenge related to secure messaging for operational communications
- Challenge related to tactical data exchange between ships in a task force
- Challenge related to shore-to-ship secure file and message transfer under bandwidth constraints

**Process**

SPRINT challenges follow the iDEX DISC process but with additional Navy-specific evaluation steps:

1. Application through iDEX portal, tagged to a SPRINT challenge
2. Technical evaluation by NIIO domain experts
3. Prototype development (funded up to Rs 1.5 Crore via iDEX, or up to Rs 10 Crore via iDEX Prime)
4. Navy-supervised sea trials
5. Operational evaluation by ship/submarine crews
6. Procurement order from the Navy

**Strategic Assessment**

SPRINT challenges offer the highest-specificity entry point. If a SPRINT challenge matches our product's capabilities, it provides a pre-defined requirement, a committed buyer (the Navy), and a structured evaluation process. The key advantage is that SPRINT challenges have VCNS-level backing, which accelerates internal Navy approvals.

### DAP 2020 (Defence Acquisition Procedure)

**What It Is**

DAP 2020 is the comprehensive procedure governing all defence acquisitions in India. It replaced the earlier DPP 2016 (Defence Procurement Procedure). DAP 2020 governs acquisitions from the initial Statement of Qualitative Requirements (SQR) through to contract signing and delivery.

**Acquisition Categories**

DAP 2020 defines several acquisition categories, listed in order of priority:

1. **Buy (Indian-IDDM)**: Indigenously Designed, Developed, and Manufactured in India. Minimum 50% indigenous content on cost basis. This is the highest priority category and receives preferential treatment in evaluation.

2. **Buy (Indian)**: Products manufactured in India with a minimum of 50% indigenous content. The design may be foreign-origin, but manufacturing must be in India.

3. **Buy and Make (Indian)**: Initial procurement from a foreign vendor, followed by Transfer of Technology (ToT) and subsequent manufacturing in India.

4. **Buy (Global)**: Direct procurement from foreign vendors. This is the lowest priority category and is used only when no Indian alternative exists.

5. **Make-I**: Government-funded development (up to 70% of development cost, capped at Rs 250 Crore). For projects requiring significant R&D.

6. **Make-II**: Industry-funded development. The developer bears all R&D costs but receives a procurement commitment upon successful development.

7. **Strategic Partnership Model**: For acquiring strategic capabilities (submarines, fighter aircraft, helicopters, etc.) through a long-term partnership between an Indian Strategic Partner and a foreign OEM.

**For Our Product: Optimal Categories**

- **Buy (Indian-IDDM)**: If we design, develop, and manufacture entirely in India, this is the strongest position. It receives a 20% price preference over Buy (Global) alternatives and is the fastest to get approved.
- **Make-II**: If we develop the product at our own cost and then offer it to the Navy, Make-II provides a structured procurement pathway with a committed buyer.

**Typical Timelines**

DAP 2020 prescribes timelines, but actual timelines vary significantly:

| Stage | Prescribed Timeline | Actual Timeline (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| Acceptance of Necessity (AoN) | 12 weeks from SQR finalization | 16 to 24 weeks |
| RFP issuance | 8 weeks from AoN | 12 to 20 weeks |
| Vendor response | 12 to 26 weeks from RFP | As prescribed |
| Technical evaluation | 18 weeks | 24 to 40 weeks |
| Field trials | 12 to 26 weeks | 26 to 52 weeks |
| Commercial negotiation | 12 weeks | 16 to 30 weeks |
| Contract signing | 8 weeks from CNC recommendation | 12 to 24 weeks |
| **Total** | **~2 to 3 years** | **3 to 7 years** |

**Strategic Assessment**

DAP 2020 is the long game. While iDEX and SPRINT provide fast-track entry, fleet-wide deployment at scale (all 130+ warships, all shore establishments) will eventually require a DAP 2020 acquisition. The optimal strategy is:

1. Win an iDEX/SPRINT challenge for initial validation and small-batch procurement
2. Demonstrate operational value through sea trials and limited deployment
3. Use the operational track record to drive a DAP 2020 Buy (Indian-IDDM) acquisition for fleet-wide deployment

### Make in India Defence Initiative

**Overview**

The Make in India initiative, launched in 2014, has a dedicated defence vertical. Key policies relevant to our product:

- **Positive Indigenisation Lists**: The MoD has released multiple lists (totaling 500+ items) of defence equipment that will not be imported after specified dates. Communication systems, messaging platforms, and cybersecurity tools appear on these lists.
- **Defence Production Corridors**: Two corridors (Tamil Nadu and Uttar Pradesh) with incentives for defence manufacturing. Relevant if hardware (ruggedized servers, terminals) is part of the product offering.
- **Defence Industrial Licensing**: Liberalized; most defence items no longer require an industrial license. Software products for defence typically do not require any specific license.
- **FDI in Defence**: Up to 74% via automatic route, 100% via government approval. Relevant if foreign investment is part of the funding strategy.

### STQC Certification Requirements

**What Is STQC**

Standardisation Testing and Quality Certification (STQC) Directorate, under the Ministry of Electronics and IT (MeitY), is the primary certification body for IT products and systems used by Indian government and defence organizations.

**Relevant Certifications**

1. **Common Criteria (CC) Certification**: STQC operates India's Common Criteria certification scheme (aligned with ISO/IEC 15408). Defence IT products typically require EAL4 or higher. For a secure communication product, EAL4+ is the likely target.

2. **FIPS 140-2 / 140-3 Equivalent**: While FIPS is a US standard, STQC recognizes and tests against equivalent cryptographic module validation requirements. Indian defence typically requires validation against "Indian Standard for Cryptographic Modules" which is aligned with FIPS 140-2 Level 2 or 3.

3. **IT Security Product Certification**: STQC's IT Security Certification Scheme (ITSCS) provides product-level certification for IT security products. This is mandatory for any cybersecurity or secure communication product sold to Indian defence.

**Timeline and Cost**

- Common Criteria EAL4+: 12 to 18 months, Rs 50 to 80 Lakh
- Cryptographic module validation: 6 to 12 months, Rs 30 to 50 Lakh
- IT Security Product Certification: 6 to 9 months, Rs 20 to 40 Lakh
- Total certification effort: 18 to 24 months if done sequentially, 12 to 15 months if parallelized

---

### BIS Standards for Defence IT

**Relevant Standards**

- **IS 15408**: Indian Standard for IT Security Evaluation Criteria (aligned with Common Criteria)
- **IS 16070**: Electronic Mail Security
- **IS/ISO 27001**: Information Security Management System (the organization developing and maintaining the product should be ISO 27001 certified)
- **IS/ISO 27701**: Privacy Information Management (relevant if the product handles personal data)
- **MIL-STD-810G (Indian equivalent under JSS-55555)**: Environmental testing for military hardware (relevant for ruggedized deployment kits)
- **JSS 50101**: Indian military standard for electronic equipment, covering shock, vibration, temperature, humidity, and EMI/EMC requirements

## Key Decision Makers in Indian Naval Communications

### Command Structure for Communications

**Chief of Naval Staff (CNS)**

The CNS is the professional head of the Indian Navy and the final authority on all capability acquisition decisions. All major procurement decisions (above Rs 500 Crore) require CNS approval. The CNS chairs the Naval Equipment Capability Board (NECB), which approves the Navy's acquisition roadmap.

**Vice Chief of Naval Staff (VCNS)**

The VCNS is the second-in-command and oversees the Navy's modernization and capability development programmes. The VCNS chairs:

- The Naval Capability Development Committee (NCDC)
- The Naval Indigenisation Advisory Committee
- NIIO reports to the VCNS

For a secure communication product, the VCNS is the most important flag officer to engage, as communications fall under the VCNS's portfolio of capability development.

**Assistant Chief of Naval Staff (ACNS) for Communications, Space, and Network Centric Operations**

This is the two-star Admiral (Rear Admiral) directly responsible for the Navy's communication strategy, space-based assets (GSAT-7/7R), and network-centric warfare capabilities. The ACNS (CSNO) is the primary technical decision-maker for any communication product.

Key responsibilities:

- Defining communication requirements (SQRs)
- Evaluating new communication technologies
- Approving communication system architectures
- Overseeing the Directorate of Naval Communications

**Directorate of Naval Communications (DNC)**

The DNC is the executing arm under ACNS (CSNO). It is headed by a Commodore or Captain-rank officer. The DNC:

- Manages the Navy's existing communication infrastructure (shore, ship, submarine, air)
- Defines technical specifications for communication equipment
- Conducts technical evaluation of vendor proposals
- Oversees installation and commissioning of communication systems on ships and shore stations
- Manages the NEWN (Navy Enterprise Wide Network) and interfaces with GSAT-7/7R operations

**Engagement Strategy**: The DNC is the first point of technical engagement. Building a relationship with the Director of Naval Communications (typically a Captain with 20+ years of communication specialization) is essential. The DNC provides:

- Insight into current pain points
- Technical requirements and constraints
- Feedback on product architecture and features
- Support during technical evaluation stages

### WESEE (Weapons and Electronics Systems Engineering Establishment)

WESEE is the Indian Navy's premier technical establishment for weapons and electronic systems, located in Mumbai. It is responsible for:

- Technical evaluation and trials of electronic systems (including communication)
- Integration of new systems onto naval platforms
- Developing test procedures and acceptance criteria
- Certifying systems for naval use

Any communication product destined for shipboard deployment will undergo WESEE evaluation. The typical WESEE evaluation process:

1. **Documentation Review**: Technical documents, design documents, security architecture, test reports
2. **Lab Testing**: Functional testing in WESEE's labs
3. **Environmental Testing**: Testing under naval environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, shock, vibration, EMI/EMC per JSS-55555)
4. **Integration Testing**: Testing integration with existing shipboard systems (CMS, ICNS, SDR-Tac)
5. **Sea Trials**: Testing aboard a designated warship under operational conditions

**Timeline**: WESEE evaluation typically takes 6 to 12 months from submission of documentation to completion of sea trials.

### BEL (Bharat Electronics Limited)

**Role**: BEL is India's largest defence electronics company and a government-owned (MoD DDP) Defence Public Sector Undertaking (DPSU). BEL occupies a unique dual role: it is simultaneously a potential partner, a potential competitor, and a potential customer.

**As a Competitor**: BEL already supplies the Navy's primary communication systems, including ICNS, SDR-Tac, and portions of the CMS (Combat Management System). See [[#competing-incumbents]] for details.

**As a Partner**: BEL frequently partners with smaller companies and startups to integrate their technology into BEL's larger system offerings. A partnership model could involve:

- BEL providing the hardware platform (ruggedized servers, terminals)
- Our product providing the secure communication software
- Joint offering under a BEL prime contract

**As a Customer**: BEL itself procures subsystems and software from other companies for integration into its products. If our secure communication product can be positioned as a subsystem within BEL's ICNS or CMS offerings, BEL becomes a direct customer.

**Strategic Assessment**: Engaging BEL early is critical. A BEL partnership de-risks the procurement process (BEL has existing contracts and relationships with the Navy) but may reduce margins. The recommended approach is to pursue iDEX/SPRINT independently for validation, then explore a BEL partnership for fleet-wide deployment.

**DEAL (Defence Electronics Applications Laboratory)**, Dehradun

- Develops communication systems, electronic warfare systems, and opto-electronic systems
- Has developed communication systems for the Army; potential for naval applications
- Key technology areas: software-defined radio, satellite communication, secure communication

**CAIR (Centre for Artificial Intelligence and Robotics)**, Bangalore

- Develops cybersecurity solutions, secure communication systems, and AI/ML applications for defence
- Has developed secure communication products including crypto devices
- Relevant for cryptographic aspects of our product

**SAG (Scientific Analysis Group)**, Delhi

- India's premier cryptography research organization within DRDO
- Develops cryptographic algorithms, key management systems, and secure communication protocols
- Any communication product for Indian defence must use SAG-approved cryptographic algorithms or undergo SAG evaluation

**NPOL (Naval Physical and Oceanographic Laboratory)**, Kochi

- Although primarily focused on sonar and underwater acoustics, NPOL has expertise in underwater communication relevant to submarine communication aspects

## Competing Incumbents

### BEL (Bharat Electronics Limited)

**Existing Naval Communication Contracts**

| System | Description | Status |
|---|---|---|
| ICNS (Integrated Communication and Navigation System) | Ship-borne integrated communication suite covering HF, VHF, UHF, SATCOM, and navigation | Deployed on all major warships |
| SDR-Tac (Software Defined Radio, Tactical) | Four-channel, multi-mode, multi-band tactical radio | Rs 490 Crore contract, 260+ units, deployed on warships and MH-60R helicopters |
| CMS (Combat Management System) | Integrates sensors, weapons, and communications on a warship | Deployed on Kolkata-class, Visakhapatnam-class destroyers |
| Sanchar | Tri-service communication terminal | Multiple variants deployed |
| LRDE (Long Range Direction Equipment) | Naval radar systems with integrated communication datalinks | Ongoing supply |

**BEL's Strengths**

- Deep institutional relationships with the Navy (50+ years)
- Existing installation base on virtually every warship
- Government-owned; inherent trust advantage
- Manufacturing capacity for ruggedized hardware
- ISO 9001, ISO 27001, CMMI Level 5 certified
- Annual revenue: approximately Rs 20,000 Crore ($2.4B)

**BEL's Weaknesses**

- Software innovation is not a core competency; BEL excels at hardware integration
- User interfaces on BEL systems are often dated and not intuitive
- BEL systems are typically purpose-built for specific communication modes; cross-mode integration (SATCOM + HF + fiber) is limited
- No modern messaging/collaboration product in BEL's portfolio
- BEL relies on DRDO/SAG for cryptographic cores; integration of modern protocols (Double Ratchet, post-quantum crypto) is slow

**Competitive Positioning Against BEL**

Our product should not position as a replacement for BEL's hardware systems (ICNS, SDR-Tac). Instead, position as a complementary software layer that sits above BEL's communication hardware:

- BEL provides the physical communication links (radio, SATCOM, fiber)
- Our product provides the secure messaging, collaboration, and data sharing application that runs over those links
- This is a "partner, not competitor" positioning that avoids triggering BEL's competitive defenses

### Thales Group

**Presence in Indian Navy**

Thales supplies several systems to the Indian Navy through offset arrangements and direct contracts:

- Sonar systems (SINTRA, Thales Underwater Systems)
- Communication systems on Scorpene-class submarines (via Naval Group, formerly DCNS)

Thales offers military-grade secure communication products (NEXIUM Defence Cloud, Citadel communications) that could theoretically be offered to the Indian Navy. However, the Indian government's push for indigenisation under "Buy Indian-IDDM" and the Positive Indigenisation Lists makes it increasingly difficult for Thales to sell standalone communication software to the Indian Navy. Thales remains relevant primarily as a subsystem supplier within larger platform contracts (e.g., Scorpene-class submarine communication suite).

## International Market Opportunities

### NATO Partner Nations

**Opportunity**

NATO nations are increasingly wary of dependence on US-controlled communication tools (Microsoft Teams, Slack) for defence communication. France (Tchap, based on Matrix) and Germany (BwMessenger, based on Matrix) have already deployed sovereign alternatives. Other NATO nations are seeking similar solutions.

**Target Countries**

| Country | Opportunity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| France | Already deployed Tchap (Matrix-based); potential interoperability partner | DINUM operates Tchap for 300,000 to 500,000 government users |
| Germany | Already deployed BwMessenger (Matrix-based); potential interoperability partner | BWI operates for Bundeswehr, targeting 500,000 users |
| Norway | Active interest in sovereign communication tools | Small navy but technology-forward |
| Netherlands | Active interest, strong naval tradition | De Zeven Provincien-class frigates |
| Poland | Modernizing armed forces with NATO accession obligations | Growing defence budget |
| Greece | Mediterranean naval power seeking alternatives | Budget constraints may favor cost-effective solutions |

**Entry Strategy**: Position as a Matrix-compatible (via Vodozemac encryption layer) naval communication product that can interoperate with Tchap and BwMessenger for allied operations while providing sovereign deployment for each nation's own fleet.

### Annual Maintenance Contract (AMC)

**Model**: After initial deployment (covered under the procurement contract), ongoing support is provided through an AMC.

- AMC rate: typically 10% to 18% of the initial contract value per year
- Includes: software updates, security patches, 24/7 support, on-site engineering support at major bases
- Duration: typically 5-year AMC contracts with renewal options

**Positioning**: The Indian Navy has approximately 70,000 personnel. At $50 to $100/user/year (Rs 4,000 to Rs 8,000/user/year), the total annual contract value would be Rs 28 to 56 Crore ($3.4M to $6.7M). This is competitive with the French and German models while being significantly cheaper than the US DEOS/NGEN-R programmes.

### Recommended Pricing Strategy

**Phase 1: iDEX/SPRINT Entry** (Year 1 to 2)

- Rs 1.5 Crore iDEX grant for prototype development
- Minimal revenue; focus on validation and operational feedback
- Target: 2 to 3 ships + 1 shore establishment for pilot deployment

**Phase 2: Limited Deployment** (Year 2 to 4)

- Rs 15 to 30 Crore contract for deployment on one fleet command (Eastern or Western Naval Command)
- Includes hardware, software, training, and 3-year AMC
- Revenue: Rs 5 to 10 Crore per year

**Phase 3: Fleet-Wide Deployment** (Year 4 to 7)

- Rs 100 to 250 Crore contract through DAP 2020 Buy (Indian-IDDM) category
- All ships, submarines, shore establishments, and naval air stations
- Revenue: Rs 25 to 50 Crore per year (including AMC)

**Phase 4: International Expansion** (Year 5+)

- Leverage Indian Navy deployment as reference customer
- Target 3 to 5 allied navies
- Revenue: Rs 50 to 150 Crore per year
